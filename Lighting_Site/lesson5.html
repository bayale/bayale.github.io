Intro:

Designing stage lighting is both a technical process and an artistic craft. In this lesson, we’ll look at how to plan and plot your design, select positions, choose colors, and balance visibility with mood to tell a story through light.

Reading and Creating a Light Plot

A light plot is a detailed diagram showing the position, type, and focus of every fixture in your design. It’s the blueprint for your lighting setup and is essential for communicating your vision to the crew. Light plots use standardized symbols for different fixture types (ellipsoidals, fresnels, PARs, movers) and include information like beam angles, gel colors, and DMX addresses.

  • Learn common symbols used for ellipsoidals, fresnels, PARs, and movers.
  • Indicate beam angles, gel colors, and DMX addresses on your plot for easy reference.
  • Always include a legend and title block with your name, date, and show title.
  • Use color coding or layers to separate different systems (front light, back light, specials, etc.).
  • Review your plot with the technical team to catch any potential issues before load-in.

Lighting Positions & Hanging Points

Choosing where to hang your lights is a critical part of the design process. Standard positions include front-of-house pipes, box booms, electrics over the stage, side ladders, and floor mounts. Each position offers unique advantages for coverage, angle, and effect.

  • Understand standard positions: front-of-house, box booms, electrics, side ladders, floor mounts, etc.
  • Match fixture types to positions based on throw distance, beam spread, and purpose.
  • Consider rigging safety, weight limits, and cable runs when planning hangs.
  • Think about sightlines and how lighting angles will affect both the performers and the audience.
  • Use side and back positions to add depth and separation, especially in dance or musical numbers.

Color Choices and Mood

Color is one of the most powerful tools in lighting design. Warm tones (reds, ambers, yellows) can evoke comfort, intimacy, or excitement, while cool tones (blues, greens, purples) suggest calm, mystery, or sadness. The choice of color should support the story, mood, and emotional arc of the production.

  • Warm vs. cool tones influence audience perception and emotional impact.
  • Use complementary colors for contrast and harmony.
  • Subtle tints can add depth without distracting from performers.
  • Plan transitions between looks for emotional flow.
  • Experiment with color mixing and layering to create unique atmospheres.
  • Consider how costume and set colors interact with your lighting choices.

Balancing Artistic and Practical Needs

Good lighting design balances visibility, atmosphere, and storytelling with the practical needs of the production. Artistic choices must work within the constraints of the venue, budget, and schedule.

  • Ensure all key areas are lit clearly for safety and performance visibility.
  • Avoid blinding the audience or washing out projection surfaces.
  • Design with quick focus and programming time in mind if schedules are tight.
  • Work closely with other departments (scenic, costumes, sound) to coordinate design choices.
  • Be flexible and ready to adapt your plan based on available equipment and crew.
  • Document your design and changes for future reference and troubleshooting.
